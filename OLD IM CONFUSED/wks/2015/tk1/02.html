Track 1: Colonized Bodies, Colonized Minds

The Science of Racism: Race, Research, and White Supremacy, Then and Now


Description

How do ways of knowing mark the racialized body as “other?” How can race-based research in the natural and social sciences be used to justify and reproduce systemic racism? And how are Asian Americans positioned in the racial hierarchy these researches support?
Scientific racism – the use of scientific techniques to support ideas of racial superiority – is woven into the fabric of American history. Notions of biologically and evolutionarily distinct races were used to justify the Atlantic slave trade and the systematic displacement of Native peoples. Asians, too, were marked as racially and civilizationally “other,” helping drive anti-Asian immigration laws like the 1882 Chinese Exclusion Act and the expanded 1924 Asian Exclusion Act.
This workshop will trace the history of scientific racism in the United States, while asking participants to identify and confront its modern legacy. With a critical historical context, we will explore how racialized arguments of biological superiority and inferiority have given way to cultural arguments (including the “model minority myth”) that continue to reproduce America’s foundational racist power structure.


Presenters

Mark Tseng Putterman

Mark Tseng Putterman is an aspiring Asian American historian and organizer. He is a Junior Research Scholar at the Asian/Pacific/American Institute at NYU, where he served as Associate Curator of Haunted Files, an exhibition and programming series dealing with the history and legacy of eugenics and scientific racism in the United States.
